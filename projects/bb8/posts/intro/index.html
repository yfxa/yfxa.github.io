Building BB-8: An AI-Powered Delivery Robot From Scratch

ROBOTICS JETSON ESP32 AI COMPUTER-VISION 3D-PRINTING

When I first saw BB-8 roll across the screen in The Force Awakens, I knew I wanted to build one. Not a toy — a functional robot that could actually do something useful. Fast forward to 2026, and that idea has evolved into something more ambitious: a BB-8-shaped indoor delivery robot that can recognize my face, understand hand gestures like "come here" and "stop," and navigate rooms on its own.

This series documents the entire build — from choosing components to writing the AI pipeline — so you can follow along or build your own.

What This Robot Does

The goal is simple in concept, complex in execution:

  • Rolls up to me when called (via app or autonomous patrol)
  • Recognizes my face using a 12MP camera and on-device ML
  • Understands gestures — open palm means stop, beckoning means come here
  • Avoids obstacles using Time-of-Flight sensors mounted inside the sphere
  • Looks like BB-8 — because if you're going to build a delivery robot, it should have personality

The head stays upright through magnetic coupling while the body rolls beneath it, just like the real thing.

The Architecture: Three Brains

The robot runs on a three-tier system, each layer handling different responsibilities:

NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano Super

The central brain. Running at 67 TOPS, it handles the heavy ML workloads: face recognition (InsightFace), gesture classification (MediaPipe), and high-level navigation decisions. It processes the camera feed and sends drive commands downstream.

ESP32-S3 #1 (Body)

Inside the rolling sphere. This handles the real-time PID control loop for the drive motors, reads the BNO055 IMU for body orientation, and polls the ToF distance sensors for obstacle data. It receives simple velocity commands from the Jetson and translates them into motor signals.

ESP32-S3 #2 (Head)

Controls the head pan/tilt servos so the head can track a person, drives status LEDs and the speaker for BB-8-style sound effects, and coordinates with the body ESP32 via ESP-NOW so the head knows which direction the body is rolling.

The Jetson talks to the body ESP32 over serial (UART). The two ESP32s talk to each other via ESP-NOW — Espressif's low-latency wireless protocol that doesn't need a Wi-Fi network. The camera on the head connects directly to the Jetson via CSI.
